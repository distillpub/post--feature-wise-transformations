Feature-wise transformations

A family of methods for fusing multiple sources of information

We often think of neural networks as a mapping from a raw source of information to a more abstract and useful representation — pixel values to object classes, audio waveform to text, etc. In practice, however, it is frequent that a task requires handling multiple sources of information; this is sometimes referred to multimodal learning.

This gives rise to the need for principled — and efficient — ways of handling multiple information sources, to fuse them, to influence (or condition) the way in which one source is processed in the context of another.

In this article, we will take a deep dive into feature-wise transformations, showing that they find their way into a surprising number of recent neural network architectures used in various problem settings. We will first discuss feature-wise transformations as a family of related approaches and show how they can be conceptualized using the Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM) nomenclature. We will then point out their numerous uses in the recent literature. Finally, we will take a stab at understanding the computational mechanisms that allow them to work.

We hope the insights contributed by this article will help highlight the strong connections between the aforementioned methods and inspire new approaches to integrating multiple information channels in neural networks.

Building towards FiLM

Imagine you're tasked with building a generative model of various classes of images (puppy, boat, airplane, etc.). The model takes a source of noise and a class as input and outputs an image sample for the requested class.

Your first instinct might be to build a separate model for every possible class. For a small number of class this is not too bad a solution, but for thousands of classes you quickly run into scaling issues: the number of parameters you're required to train and store is multiplied by the number of classes. You are also missing out on the opportunity to leverage commonalities between classes: for instance, different types of dogs (puppy, terrier, dalmatian, etc.) share common visual traits and are likely to share computation when mapping from the abstract noise vector to the output image.

Now imagine that in addition to the various classes you are also required to take into account various attributes, like size or color. In that case, you can't reasonably expect to train a separate network for all possible conditioning values! Let's examine a few options.

A simple fix would be to concatenate a representation of the conditioning information to the noise vector and treat the result as the model's input. This is very parameter-efficient — all we need to do is to increase the size of the first layer's weight matrix to accomodate the conditioning representation — but the deeper we get into the network, the more diffuse and indirect the conditioning information's influence becomes.

No problem, you think: let's simply concatenate the conditioning representation to the input of all layers in the network! We call this concatenation-based conditioning:

One advantage repeated concatenation has over just concatenating to the input of the first (or sometimes last) layer is that you don't really need to choose when is the best time to integrate the conditioning information into the network; you let the network figure it out while it trains. Maybe you want to give yourself options, though: concatenation works fine, but are there other mechanisms to use? What about biasing the hidden layers of your network based on the conditioning representation?

As it turns out, conditional biasing is an equivalent — and computationally more efficient — way of implementing concatenation-based conditioning. To understand the equivalence, consider a simple fully-connected linear layer applied to the concatenation of an input \mathbf{x} and a conditioning representation \mathbf{z}. The same argument applies to convolutional networks, provided we ignore the border effects due to zero-padding.

Each vector product between \mathbf{z} and its corresponding subset of a row of the weight matrix can be thought of as a bias that gets added to the corresponding output feature. How about multiplicative interactions, then?

A special instance of this is feature-wise sigmoidal gating: each feature or feature map is scaled by a scalar between 0 and 1 as a function of the conditioning representation. This constraint is often enforced via a sigmoidal layer, hence the name. Intuitively, feature-wise sigmoidal gating allows the conditioning information to select which features are passed forward and which are zeroed out.

Given that conditioning with additive interactions seems natural and intuitive, why use multiplicative interactions? Multiplicative interactions are useful in learning relationships between inputs, as these interactions natural identify “matches”; elements with highly similar values (regardless of sign) will generally produce higher values when multiplied and lower values otherwise. This feature is why dot products are often used to determine how similar two vectors are. In contrast, additive interactions are more natural for functions that are less strongly dependent on the joint values of two inputs, such as feature aggregation or feature detection (i.e. checking if a feature is present in either of two inputs). Multiplicative interactions alone have had a history of success in various domains. — see Bibliographic Notes.

This brings us to the combination of conditional scaling and conditional shifting into a conditional feature-wise affine transformation, An affine transformation is a transformation of the form y = m * x + b. which acts as an umbrella for all approaches discussed so far and may retain the advantages of each. In general, feature-wise transformations enjoy very good scaling properties — their number of parameters scales linearly with the total number of features in the network — and as we will see, they work remarkably well as a conditioning mechanism despite their simplicity.

FiLM

To center the discussion around a common language, we propose to use the nomenclature of Perez et al. , which formalizes feature-wise affine transformations under the acronym FiLM, for Feature-wise Linear Modulation. Strictly speaking, linear is a misnomer, as we allow biasing, but we hope the more rigorous-minded reader will forgive us for the sake of a better-sounding acronym.

We say that a neural network is modulated using FiLM, or FiLM-ed, through the insertion of FiLM layers in its architecture. These are parametrized by some form of conditioning information, and the mapping from conditioning information to FiLM parameters is called the FiLM generator. In other words, the FiLM generator is going to predict the parameters of the FiLM layers based on some arbitrary input. For simplicity, you can assume that the FiLM generator outputs the concatenation of all FiLM parameters for the network architecture.

As the name implies, a FiLM layer applies a feature-wise affine transformation to its input. By feature-wise, we mean that scaling and shifting are applied element-wise, or in the case of convolutional networks, feature map-wise. To expand a little more on the convolutional case, feature maps can be thought of as the same feature detector being evaluated at different spatial locations, in which case it makes sense to apply the same affine transformation to all spatial locations. In other words, assuming \mathbf{x} is a FiLM layer's input and \mathbf{z} is the conditioning information, and assuming \gamma and \beta are \mathbf{z}-dependent scaling and shifting vectors, \textrm{FiLM}(\mathbf{x}) = \gamma(\mathbf{z}) \odot \mathbf{x} + \beta(\mathbf{z}), You can interact with the following fully-connected and convolutional FiLM layers to get an intuition of the sort of modulation they allow:

Connecting FiLM to other approaches

FiLM shares with HyperNetworks the architectural feature that a secondary neural network computes some of the parameter values in the primary network. From that perspective, the FiLM generator can be viewed as a specialized instance of a HyperNetwork which predicts the FiLM parameters of the FiLM-ed network. In fact, there exists a spectrum of methods ranging from FiLM (which predicts a tiny subset of a network's parameters) to HyperNetworks (which predicts all network parameters) — see Bibliographic Notes. FiLM layers require predicting far fewer parameters than Hypernetworks, but is less flexible in its ability to modulate the model's computation. The ideal trade-off between flexibility and computational complexity is still an ongoing area of investigation.

Some parallels can be drawn between FiLM and attention, but the two operate in different ways which we think are important to disambiguate.

This difference stems from two different intuitions underlying attention and FiLM: for attention, it is assumed that the useful information is contained in specific spatial locations or time steps, whereas for FiLM, it is assumed that the useful information is contained in specific features or feature maps. In the end, FiLM and attention can be seen as two complementary tools in the neural toolbox.

Finally, FiLM layers can be seen as a special case of a bilinear transformation with low-rank weight matrices. Bilinear transformations themselves have a long history in the machine learning literature — see Bibliographic Notes. A bilinear transformation defines the relationship between two inputs \mathbf{x} and \mathbf{z} and the k^{th} output feature y_k as y_k = \mathbf{x}^T W_k \mathbf{z}. Note that for each output feature y_k we have a separate matrix W_k, so the full set of weights forms a 3-tensor.

Feature-wise transformations in the literature

Feature-wise transformations find their way in lots of problem settings, but because of their very general nature and wide applicability, this connection is seldom drawn. Here are notable examples, grouped by application domain.

Generative modeling

Concatenation-based conditioning is used in the conditional variant of DCGAN , a well-recognized class of network architectures for generative adversarial networks (GANs) . The class label is broadcasted as a feature map and is concatenated to the input of convolutional and transposed convolutional layers in the generator and discriminator networks.

Conditional biasing is used by PixelCNN and WaveNet , two recent advances in autoregressive modeling of image and audio data, respectively. In its simplest form, conditioning in PixelCNN is achieved by adding feature-wise, conditional biases to the output of all convolutional layers. In FiLM parlance, this means inserting FiLM layers after each convolutional layer and setting the scaling coefficients to a constant value of 1. The authors also describe a location-dependent biasing scheme which cannot be expressed in terms of FiLM layers due to the absence of the feature-wise property.

WaveNet describes two ways in which conditional biasing allows external information to modulate the behavior of the model:

  1. Global conditioning applies the same conditional bias to the whole generated sequence and is used e.g. to condition on speaker identity.
  2. Local conditioning applies a conditional bias which varies across time steps of the generated sequence and is used e.g. to let linguistic features in a text-to-speech (TTS) model influence which sounds are produced.

Like for PixelCNN, both approaches can be viewed as inserting FiLM layers after each convolutional layer. The main difference lies in the way in which the FiLM-generating network is defined: global conditioning expresses the FiLM-generating network as an embedding lookup which is broadcasted to the whole time series, whereas local conditioning expresses it as a mapping from an input sequence of conditioning information to an output sequence of FiLM parameters.

Reinforcement learning

Feature-wise sigmoidal gating is used in the Gated-Attention architecture to fuse linguistic and visual information in an agent trained to follow simple natural language instructions in the VizDoom 3D environment.

Feature-wise affine transformations are used in Kirkpatrick et al. to condition the policy of an agent trained to play 10 different Atari games on the game being played. More specifically, game-specific gains and biases are used in a shared policy network, which can be viewed as inserting FiLM layers throughout its hierarchy.

Natural language processing

Feature-wise scaling is used in the Gated-Attention Reader . The model extracts information from a text by conditioning the document reading network on a query. Its architecture is formed of multiple Gated-Attention modules, which involve element-wise multiplications between document representation tokens and token-specific query representations extracted via soft attention on the query representation tokens.

Sigmoidal gating is used by Dauphin et al. in their proposed gated linear unit, which use half of the input features to apply feature-wise sigmoidal gating to the second half. This architectural feature is also adopted by Gehring et al. , who introduce a fast and parallelizable model for machine translation in the form of a purely convolutional architecture.

Visual question-answering

Figure-wise affine transformations are used by Perez et al. to build a visual reasoning model trained on the CLEVR dataset to answer synthetic questions about a synthetic input image.

The model features a FiLM generator in the form of a linguistic pipeline extracting a question representation which is then linearly mapped to the FiLM parameter values. The visual pipeline is conditioned by inserting a FiLM layer in the residual pathway of each residual block of the convolutional neural network. The model is trained end-to-end on image-question-answer tuples.

de Vries et al. leverage FiLM to condition a pre-trained network. The visual pipeline of a visual question-answering (VQA) model is modulated by the model's linguistic pipeline through conditional batch normalization (CBN), which can be viewed as a special case of FiLM. The model is trained on the "oracle" sub-task of the GuessWhat?! task — which features natural language questions on images answerable by yes, no, or N/A — as well as the VQAv1 dataset.

The visual pipeline consists of a pre-trained residual network and is kept fixed throughout training. The linguistic pipeline interacts with the visual pipeline by outputting perturbations to the residual network's batch normalization parameters. Like hinted at earlier, CBN can be viewed as an instance of FiLM where the post-normalization feature-wise affine transformation is replaced with a FiLM layer.

Style transfer

Feature-wise transformations are used in Dumoulin et al. to condition a style transfer network on a chosen style image. Conditional instance normalization (CIN) layers are introduced into a fast feedforward style transfer network to accommodate multiple styles. Each style modeled by the network is associated with its own set of instance normalization parameters, and conditioning is achieved by applying the normalization with style-specific parameters.

This intuition is pushed further by Ghiasi et al. with the introduction of a style prediction network which is trained jointly with the style transfer network to predict the instance normalization parameters directly from a given style image.

Like CBN, CIN can be seen as an instance of FiLM where the post-normalization feature-wise affine transformation is replaced with a FiLM layer. The main differentiator between Dumoulin et al. and Ghiasi et al. is the FiLM generator implementation: in the former the generator is a simple embedding lookup, whereas in the latter the generator is a convolutional neural network.

So far, the models we covered have two sub-networks: a primary network in which feature-wise transformations are applied, and a secondary network which outputs parameters for these transformations. However, this distinction between FiLM-ed network and FiLM generator is not strictly necessary. As an example, Huang et al. proposes an alternative for fast and arbitrary style transfer in the form of an adaptive instance normalization (AdaIN) layer, which computes the normalization parameters using a simple heuristic.

The content and style images are first fed through the model up to the AdaIN layer. The stack of content feature maps is then instance-normalized, and the mean and standard deviation statistics of the stack of style feature maps are used to parametrize the post-normalization affine transformation. AdaIN can be interpreted as inserting a FiLM layer midway through the model architecture. However, rather than relying on a secondary network to predict the FiLM parameters from the style image, the main network itself is used to extract style features which are used to compute the FiLM parameters. Therefore, the model can be seen as both the FiLM-ed network the FiLM generator.

Computer vision

As we have seen so far, feature-wise transformations can be used as a conditioning mechanism to incorporate external information such as context representation, task description, class information, or user information. However, there is nothing preventing us from considering a neural network's hidden activations themselves as conditioning information. This idea gives rise to various forms of self-conditioned models.

A prime example of this principle being applied can be found in Highway Networks , which take inspiration from the LSTMs' heavy use of feature-wise sigmoidal gating in their input, forget, and output gates to regulate information flow:

The ImageNet 2017 winning model employs a self-conditioning scheme in the form of feature-wise sigmoidal gating as a way to "recalibrate" a layer's activations conditioned on themselves.

Speech recognition

In the speech recognition domain, Kim et al. modulates a deep bidirectional LSTM using a form of conditional normalization which the authors call dynamic layer normalization. In analogy to conditional instance normalization and conditional batch normalization, dynamic layer normalization can be seen as an instance of FiLM where the post-normalization feature-wise affine transformation is disabled and a FiLM layer is placed immediately after the instance normalization layer.

The key difference here is that the conditioning signal does not come from an external source, but is rather computed from utterance summarization feature vectors extracted in each layer of the network as a way of adapting the neural acoustic model.

Properties of FiLM-ed networks

If you are like us, you probably find the success of feature-wise transformations intriguing given how simple they are. It appears that modulating the values of FiLM parameters — which we reiterate are a tiny subset of the model's parameters — is sufficient to have drastic effects on the computational behaviour of the FiLM-ed network.

In adopting the FiLM perspective, we implicitly introduce a notion of task representation: each task — be it a question about an image or a painting style to imitate — elicits a different set of FiLM parameters via the FiLM generator which can be understood as its representation in terms of how to modulate the FiLMed network. This representation can be leveraged in various ways.

We can use it to "interpolate" between tasks. For instance, we can generate stylizations that are in-between two different paintings by interpolating between their respective FiLM parameter values.

We can use it to create task analogies. For example, in a visual reasoning setting we can make successful predictions for questions that involves combinations of concepts that have not been seen during training. If the question can be expressed in terms of three questions that have already been seen (e.g., "What is the blue big cylinder made of?" = "What is the blue big sphere made of?" + "What is the green big cylinder made of?" - "What is the green big sphere made of?"), then we can combine the FiLM parameters associated with the three questions in the same way to obtain the FiLM parameters that answer the new question.

We can use it to gain insights into the relationship between tasks, for instance by applying dimensionality reduction techniques on the FiLM parameter values.

In light of these observations, we wonder: how are feature-wise affine transformations able to perform these sorts of modulations on the FiLM-ed network?

A tale of two networks

We began this analysis with a hypothesis: FiLM allows us to implement soft switching. Intuitively, this means that a FiLM generator acts as as compiler of sorts. It maps from a high-level task description to a set of parameters that describe how information should flow through the network.

To test this hypothesis we measure the effect different task descriptions have on the activations of the networks in question. We do this by computing forward passes of the network and measuring the magnitude (Frobenius norm) of each channel activations.

These examples show that there is a fairly sparse activation pattern, and that it shifts with the task description. There are however two major caveats that somewhat invalidate our working hypothesis.

First, although the patterns are somewhat sparse, there are relatively few full zeroed out channels. We considered that the FiLM generator could learn to use the ReLU-ed activation function completely zero out certain filters. Approximately 9% of the channels are completely switched off for the FiLM-CLEVR neural network, but it isn’t the effect we had initially predicted.

Second, while there is some amount of switching—how else could the network complete the task description—there are also a number of channels that remain on consistently! Is this a fluke? Perhaps this set of examples happen to all utilize the same ‘soft program’. To determine this lets measure these activation magnitudes across the entire validation set.

Notice the prominent horizontal bands! From this height it appears that channels are mostly on, or mostly off. Looking closely we can see the switching effects but they appear overpowered. We compute the mean and standard deviation over the validation set.

The means show the increase in the signal magnitude as the network depth increases.

The standard deviations show us that while channels are activated more overall, the intensity of the switching increases as well.

Where does this leave us? Our hypothesis is not clearly falsified nor validated by these observations. We observe some soft-switching effects, but there remains a distributed effect. While the FiLM generator learns to switch channels off completely with ReLU /some/ of the time, it’s minor in comparison to the many, low-magnitude channel activations.

Part of this effect is explained by the observation that many task descriptions will require the same information carry through till the end. And in the style transfer case, a great deal of information must be propagated as we’d like the content to remain invariant in some way.

Further questions: recent Distill work on feature visualization allows us to probe into the semantics of these feature maps. - What do the channels that are consistently activated do? - What do the channels that are switch highly do? - If we replace this soft-switching mechanism with a binary predictor can we get the same results?

Conclusion

This article had quite a lot of ground to cover. We set out to better understand feature-wise transformations in the context of fusing multiple sources of information in a neural network. We examined several approaches which are encompassed by the FiLM formulation, and reviewed their use in the literature. We hope that the recognition of the wide range of applications feature-wise transformations found in the literature will stimulate research into applying them to even more problem settings.

Bibliographic Notes

Multiplicative interactions have succeeded on various tasks, ever since they were introduced in computer vision as "mapping units" and "dynamic mappings" around 40 years ago. These tasks include Action Recognition , Tracking , Pose Estimation, Denoising, Character-level Language Modeling, and, more generally, tasks involving relating or matching inputs, such as from different modalities or points in time.

Many models lie on the spectrum between FiLM and Hypernetworks:

  • Adaptive CNN (ACNN) predicts the value of several of the model's convolution filters as a function of auxiliary inputs like camera perspective, level of noise, etc. The resulting convolution filters turn out to be very effective in difficult vision tasks such as crowd counting or image deblurring.
  • Residual Adapters also propose to predict entire convolutional filters conditioned on the visual recognition domain they are operating in.
  • In the zero-shot/one-shot learning setting, the model proposed by Ba et al. predicts convolutional filters and classifiers weights based on textual descriptions of object classes.
  • In the reinforcement learning setting, the model proposed by Oh et al. computes the parameters of a policy-convolutional network conditioned on the task description.

Bilinear models were first introduced in the vision community by Tenenbaum et al. to better untangle latent perceptual factors. The authors wanted to separate the image style from its content, arguing that classic linear models were not rich enough to extract such complex interaction. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by applying it for spoken vowel identification or zero-shot font classification. Notable applications include:

  • Chuang et al. perform facial animation using bilinear transformations by separating key facial features (the style) from visual emotions (the content). This allows to modify a sequence originally recorded with a happy expression so that the subject appears to be speaking with an angry or neutral expression.
  • Chu et al. and Yang et al. apply bilinear models to recommendation systems by extracting user and item information in various settings. More generally, recommendation systems rely heavily on matrix factorization methods , which can be viewed as a bilinear model in which one of the latent vectors is kept fixed.
  • More recently, bilinear models have inspired new neural architecture in visual recognition , video action recognition , and visual question-answering.
